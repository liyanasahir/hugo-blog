*A field guide to brewing your own flat white instead of buying it, and when you should still buy it anyway.*

***

You have never used a moka pot. Someone gifted you one, and we went down a rabbit hole figuring out how it works, what it costs to brew at home vs. going to a cafe, and whether the whole thing is even worth the effort.

Here is everything we found. Expect bad coffee at first.

***

## How the pot actually works

This is the part that took the longest to understand, and no one explains it simply.

**"240 ml" is the most coffee the pot makes.** Not the water you add. That is six 40 ml espresso-style cups. A classic "6-cup" moka.

**You don't choose the dose. The basket does.** Fill it level. No tamping, no heaping. That fixed amount (around 18 g, somewhere in the 17-22 g range) is what you use every time. Blue Tokai says "16 g per 120 ml." A full pot would need ~38 g. The basket can not hold that. The geometry wins.

Tip a level-full basket onto a kitchen scale and read the number. Now you know your pot instead of trusting a guess.

**You can not make less than a full pot.** Under-fill the basket and water channels through the gap. You get something weak, bitter, and uneven. The moka is built to run full. For one person, that is a lot. For two, it is perfect.

## The actual trade-off

Before the numbers: this is the thing that reframed everything.

Home time is pure cost. Labour you would rather skip. Cafe time is the product. The sitting, the service, the change of scene. That Rs. 272 at Blue Tokai is not overpriced coffee. It is rent on a room and an hour of being looked after.

So this is not a fight. It is a division of labour.

| The Cafe | The Pot |
|---|---|
| Real 9-bar espresso with steamed microfoam | Strong coffee at ~1.5-2 bar, no true crema |
| Atmosphere, focus, you are being served | A quiet ritual at your own counter |
| Zero labour, zero cleanup | Prep + cleanup friction every single time |
| ~Rs. 252-272 a cup | ~Rs. 33-56 a cup |
| A soft 1-hour social ceiling per drink | No time limit. Linger, refill, stay all morning |
| You buy a place to *be* | You build a skill that compounds daily |

Two drinks in one sitting: ~Rs. 111 (oat) / ~Rs. 65 (dairy) at home, vs Rs. 544 / Rs. 504 at the outlet.

### Two things worth noting

1. **Oat milk is the biggest cost at home, not coffee.** At Rs. 32, the milk costs more than the coffee in an oat flat white. Oat vs dairy swings your cup by ~40%. The milk is the only real lever.
2. **Even at the cheapest bean, the saving is massive.** Rs. 650 for 250 g sounds steep until you do the per-cup math. Rs. 23.50 vs Rs. 252. The money is in the milk, not the bean.

## The leftover coffee

The grounds are paid for the moment you brew. So a third drink from the leftover ~80-120 ml costs only its milk. Nothing if you take it black. A third iced cup drops your per-cup coffee cost from Rs. 23.50 to ~Rs. 15.70.

**What to do with the extra:**
- **Iced in the afternoon.** Decant into a jar immediately, refrigerate, pour over ice. Moka coffee is apparently great cold. This is the highest-value move.
- **Coffee ice cubes.** Freeze it. Future iced coffees never get watered down.
- **Affogato, baking, overnight oats.** Anywhere strong cold coffee works as an ingredient.

**Two rules that come up everywhere:**
- Decant immediately. Do not leave it on the warm hob. It keeps cooking and turns bitter.
- Never reheat. Reheating flattens everything. Leftovers go cold or iced. Sealed in the fridge, good for about 24 hours.

## The time and friction (two drinks)

| Step | Time | Hands-on? |
|---|---|---|
| Fill, level the basket, assemble | ~2 min | yes |
| Brew on the hob | ~5 min | semi. Pull it at the gurgle |
| Froth milk for two | ~2-3 min | yes |
| Pour and build | ~1 min | yes |
| Cleanup (knock out puck, rinse 3 parts + frother) | ~2-3 min | yes |

About 12 minutes total. Roughly 8 minutes hands-on. For two drinks.

**The catches:**
- The moka will not let you walk away. Pull it the second it sputters or it over-extracts. No timer, no wandering off.
- Cleanup is the real tax. Not hard, but daily and recurring. Recurring friction is what quietly kills habits. (Rinse the aluminium only. No soap. It leaves a taste.)
- You are the barista now, and a beginner. The cafe does 9-bar espresso with microfoam. The moka does ~1.5-2 bar, no real crema, and a handheld frother gives decent but coarser foam. The gap is real. It narrows with practice. It will not close on this gear.

The first few pots will probably be terrible. That is part of the deal.

***

## Where this lands

Home is not competing with the cafe. It is competing with *not having good coffee at home*. For the daily, functional, get-the-day-started cup, it wins: ~6 minutes, no leaving the house, Rs. 33-56, on demand, no closing hours.

The cafe wins when being there is the point. The atmosphere. The focus. The hour with someone. Home does not replace that at any price because it is not the same thing.

Brew for the daily cups. The saving compounds. Go to the cafe for the outing, and price it honestly as buying the room, not the coffee.

> The highest-leverage upgrade is not a better bean or a fancier frother. It is killing the cleanup friction. A drying rack by the hob, a fixed routine, washing up while the milk froths. Lower that barrier and the habit sticks. Leave it annoying and you quietly drift back to Rs. 272 a cup.
