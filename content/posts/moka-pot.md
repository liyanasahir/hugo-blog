*Or: what a 240 ml aluminium pot taught me about coffee, money, and friction.*

*A field guide to brewing your own flat white instead of buying it, and when you should still buy it anyway.*

***

## The one-line verdict

Brewing at home beats the cafe on price by roughly **5x (oat milk)** to **8x (dairy)**, and that's before you remember the pot was a gift. But the cafe was never selling you coffee. It was selling you a room, a barista, and an hour of being looked after. So this isn't a fight. It's a division of labour.

***

## The gear

- **The pot:** Agaro Classic Moka Pot, 240 ml. Gifted, so its cost is **Rs. 0**. (Retails Rs. 799.)
- **The bean:** Blue Tokai *Dhak Blend*, pre-ground. **Rs. 650 / 250 g = Rs. 2.60 per gram.**
- **The milk:** Alt Co oat (Rs. 265/L = Rs. 0.265/ml) *or* dairy (Rs. 34 / 450 ml = Rs. 0.076/ml).

***

## How the pot actually works (the part nobody tells you)

**"240 ml" is the most coffee it makes**, not the water you add. That's six 40 ml espresso-style cups, a classic "6-cup" moka.

**You don't choose the dose. The basket does.** Fill it level, no tamping, no heaping, and that fixed amount (~**18 g**, somewhere in the 17-22 g range) is what you use *every single time*. Blue Tokai's "16 g per 120 ml" ratio simply can't be honoured here: a full pot would demand ~38 g, and the basket physically can't hold it. The geometry wins.

> **Do this once:** tip a level-full basket onto a kitchen scale and read the number. Now you *know* your pot instead of trusting an estimate.

**You can't really make less than a full pot.** Under-fill the basket and water channels through the gap, giving you something weak, bitter, and uneven. The moka is built to run full. For one person that's a problem. For two, it's perfect.

## The money (one full pot, two flat whites)

The full pot costs **Rs. 47 in grounds** no matter what. Split between two drinks, that's **Rs. 23.50 of coffee per cup.**

| Per flat white | Oat (Alt Co) | Dairy |
|---|---:|---:|
| Coffee (half pot) | Rs. 23.50 | Rs. 23.50 |
| Milk (~120 ml) | Rs. 32.00 | Rs. 9.00 |
| **Home total** | **~Rs. 56** | **~Rs. 33** |
| Blue Tokai outlet | Rs. 272 | Rs. 252 |
| **You save** | **~Rs. 216** | **~Rs. 219** |
| **Cheaper by** | **~4.9x** | **~7.7x** |

**Two drinks in one sitting:** ~Rs. 111 (oat) / ~Rs. 65 (dairy) at home, vs Rs. 544 / Rs. 504 at the outlet.

### Two things that surprised me

1. **Oat milk, not coffee, is the biggest cost at home.** At Rs. 32, the milk costs *more than the coffee* in an oat flat white. Oat vs dairy swings your cup by ~40%. It's the only real lever.
2. **The premium bean barely matters per cup.** A cheaper Rs. 450 blend would save only ~Rs. 3-4 a drink. So drink Dhak because you *love* Dhak, not to save money. The money's in the milk.

***

## The leftover coffee (it's free, actually)

The grounds are paid for the moment you brew. So a third drink from the surplus ~80-120 ml costs **only its milk, or nothing if you take it black.** A third iced cup drops your coffee-per-cup from Rs. 23.50 to ~Rs. 15.70.

**Best uses for the extra:**
- **Iced in the afternoon.** Decant into a jar *immediately*, refrigerate, pour over ice. Moka coffee is gorgeous cold. Highest-value move.
- **Coffee ice cubes.** Freeze it; future iced coffees never get watered down.
- **Affogato, baking, overnight oats, smoothies.** Anywhere strong cold coffee is an input.

**Two rules:**
- **Decant immediately.** Don't leave it stewing on the warm hob; it keeps cooking and turns bitter.
- **Never reheat it.** Reheating flattens and embitters. Always take leftovers cold or iced. Sealed in the fridge, it's good ~24 hours.

## The time and friction ledger (two drinks)

| Step | Time | Hands-on? |
|---|---|---|
| Fill, level the basket, assemble | ~2 min | yes |
| Brew (~5 min on the hob) | ~5 min | semi, pull it at the gurgle |
| Froth milk for two | ~2-3 min | yes |
| Pour and build | ~1 min | yes |
| Cleanup (knock out puck, rinse 3 parts + frother) | ~2-3 min | yes |

**Around 12 min wall-clock, roughly 8 min truly hands-on, for two drinks.**

**The catches, named honestly:**
- **The moka won't let you wander.** Pull it the second it sputters or it over-extracts into bitterness. No timer, no walking away.
- **Cleanup is the real tax.** Not hard, but *daily and recurring*, and recurring friction is what quietly kills habits. (Rinse the aluminium only. No soap; it leaves a taste.)
- **You're the barista now, and a rookie one.** The cafe pulls real **9-bar espresso** with steamed microfoam. Your moka makes strong coffee at **~1.5-2 bar**, no true crema, and a handheld frother gives decent-but-coarser foam. The gap is real. It *narrows* with practice; it won't fully close on this gear.

***

## The actual trade-off

The mistake is comparing the two *times* as if they're the same thing. **Home time is pure cost**, labour you'd rather skip. **Cafe time is the product**, the sitting, the service, the change of scene. The Rs. 272 isn't overpriced coffee; it's rent on a room and an hour of being looked after.

| The Cafe | The Pot |
|---|---|
| Top-notch quality (9-bar, microfoam) | Strong, characterful, ~1.5-2 bar, yours to improve |
| Atmosphere + the focus it gives you | Quiet, meditative ritual at your own counter |
| Zero labour, zero cleanup, you're served | Prep + cleanup friction (the habit's main barrier) |
| ~Rs. 252-272 a cup | ~Rs. 33-56 a cup |
| A soft 1-hour-per-drink social ceiling | No time limit, linger, refill, stay all morning |
| You buy a place to *be* | You build a skill that compounds daily |

***

## The rule I'm landing on

Home isn't competing with the cafe. It's competing with *not having good coffee at home*, and for the daily, functional, get-the-day-started cup, it wins outright: ~6 minutes, no leaving, Rs. 33-56, on demand, no closing hours, no hour-glass on the table.

The cafe wins on the days when **being there is the point**: the atmosphere, the focus, the hour with someone that isn't at your own kitchen sink. Home doesn't replace that at any price, because it isn't the same thing.

So: **brew for the daily cups, where the saving lives and compounds. Go to the outlet for the outing, and price the outing honestly as buying the room, not the coffee.**

> **The highest-leverage upgrade isn't a better bean or a fancier frother. It's killing the cleanup friction.** A drying rack by the hob, a fixed routine, washing-up while the milk froths. Lower that one barrier and the home habit sticks. Leave it annoying and you'll quietly drift back to Rs. 272 a cup.

***

### Confidence notes (because we keep ourselves honest)
- **Prices, milk costs, cafe prices:** high confidence, straight from the sources.
- **Basket dose (~18 g):** moderate. Agaro lists no grams; *weigh it to be sure*.
- **Time estimates:** moderate, yours will shift with practice and setup.
- **9-bar vs ~1.5-2 bar, "home wins on cost":** high confidence.
- **Per-cup figures assume two drinks per pot** (the surplus makes home *cheaper* still, never more expensive).
